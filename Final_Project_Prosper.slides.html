Bivariate Exploration¶

The first thing I looked into was how did the loan amount affect interest rates. In the following graph we can see that the more you borrow, the lower your rate tends to be. Now this isn't super encouraging because large amounts of debt are still bad. However, it is good to know that the highest ranges of interest rates are typically not being applied to the people with the highest amounts of debt being consolidated!

Next I looked at various factors that might affect interest rates and how being a homeowner factors into that as well. We can see that for all but two of these graphs that being a homeowner doesn't seem to make a big difference at all.

However, when looking at Debt to Income Ratio and Revolving Credit Balance it seems to be correlated to higher interest rates. I initially believed that being a homeowner would be helpful to interest rates in every case but when seeing these two categories it makes sense that owning a home increases your debt burden and in many cases your expenses which could easily lead to higher revolving credit balances in the months or years leading up to debt consolidation.

I wanted to look further into if there were any positives associated with homeownership and interest rates so I compared with employment rate. To our homeowners benefit in all categories, except unemployment, they can expect lower rates compared to not owning a home.

Again this makes sense as being unemployed and needing to make a home payment puts your in a more precarious position that is cause for a higher rate from Prosper.

What did we learn?¶

True to convential wisdom, if you want a lower interest rate the best things you can do seem to be:

  • have a high credit score
  • have low amounts of revolving credit
  • be employed and own a home while recognizing that owning may cost you in higher interest if you find yourself unemployed
